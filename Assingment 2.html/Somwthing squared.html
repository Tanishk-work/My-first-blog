Computer viruses come in various forms and can manifest in different ways, depending on their design and intended purpose. Some viruses are relatively benign and merely cause nuisance or inconvenience by displaying pop-up messages or slowing down system performance. However, others are more insidious and can inflict severe damage by corrupting or deleting files, stealing sensitive information, or providing unauthorized access to the infected system. Moreover, certain viruses are programmed to evade detection and removal by employing sophisticated techniques, such as encryption, polymorphism, and rootkit capabilities, making them particularly challenging to identify and eradicate. Computer viruses are malicious software programs designed to replicate and spread from one computer system to another, often with the intention of causing harm or disruption. These digital parasites attach themselves to legitimate files or programs and execute malicious code when activated, potentially compromising the security and functionality of the infected system. Computer viruses can be transmitted through various means, including email attachments, infected websites, removable storage devices, and network connections, posing a significant threat to individuals, businesses, and organizations worldwide. One of the defining characteristics of computer viruses is their ability to replicate and spread autonomously, often without the user's knowledge or consent. Once a computer virus infects a system, it can propagate itself by infecting other files or programs, allowing it to spread rapidly across multiple devices and networks. This self-replicating behavior enables viruses to quickly proliferate and wreak havoc on a large scale, leading to widespread system failures, data loss, and other adverse consequences. Additionally, some viruses are designed to remain dormant after infecting a system, lying in wait until triggered by specific conditions or commands before unleashing their destructive payloads.
